Oscilloscope — What it Does

Learn the function and parts of an oscilloscope before trying the interactive simulation.

What is an Oscilloscope?

An oscilloscope is an electronic instrument that graphically displays varying signal voltages, usually as a 2D plot of voltage (vertical axis) versus time (horizontal axis). It allows engineers and students to visualize and analyze waveform shapes, amplitude, frequency, rise/fall times, noise, and other characteristics.

Key Functions

  • Visualize waveforms: See how voltage changes over time.
  • Measure amplitude & frequency: Read peak-to-peak, RMS, period, and frequency.
  • Triggering: Stabilize repetitive waveforms so they appear steady on screen.
  • Cursors & math: Apply basic measurements or analysis like FFT (in advanced models).
  • Capture transients: Capture single-shot or rare events with proper acquisition modes.

Common Parts

Probe
Connects the circuit under test to the oscilloscope input.
Vertical
Controls for voltage scale (volts/div) and channel position.
Horizontal
Controls time base (time/div) and horizontal position.
Trigger
Sets the condition to start waveform capture.
Display
The screen showing the waveform grid.

Quick Examples

  • Sine wave: Clean periodic signal — used for audio and AC power tests.
  • Square wave: Fast edges — for digital logic timing.
  • Ringing: Shows unwanted oscillations after transitions.
  • Sawtooth wave: Gradual rise then sharp drop — used in audio and timing circuits.
  • Noise: Small rapid fluctuations — grounding or EMI issues.
